Cleft Sentences & Emphasis (It was… that…)

Lesson 10 · ~12 minutes · Advanced emphasis structures

By the end of this lesson, you'll be able to use cleft sentences to redirect emphasis in English — the kind of move that makes your speech sound more natural, persuasive, and clearly B2-level. You'll also know when not to use them.

Why this matters

Most learners say: "John stole the money." That's fine. But what if you want to stress who did it — not the stealing, not the money, but John? Native speakers use cleft sentences to do this naturally: "It was John who stole the money." The sentence means the same thing, but the emphasis lands on a different word. This is a hallmark of fluent, nuanced English.

中文思维差异：中文靠重读或加"是…的"来强调，但结构不变。比如"是小王偷的钱"。英语用 cleft sentence 把句子"劈开"（cleft = split），重组结构来突出某个成分。这是从 B1 跳到 B2 的关键技能之一。

The It-Cleft

The it-cleft is the most common type. It splits a simple sentence into two parts: an it clause and a that/who clause. The emphasized element goes right after it.

Formula: It + be + [emphasized element] + that/who + [rest of sentence]

Emphasizing the subject

It + be + person/thing + who/that + verb + object

  • Normal: Sarah wrote this report.
  • Cleft: It was Sarah who/that wrote this report. (Not someone else.)

Emphasizing the object

It + be + object + that + subject + verb

  • Normal: They fixed the server last night.
  • Cleft: It was the server that they fixed last night. (Not something else.)

Emphasizing time or place

It + be + time/place + that + [rest of sentence]

  • Normal: We discovered the bug in production.
  • Cleft: It was in production that we discovered the bug. (Not in staging.)
  • Normal: She resigned in January.
  • Cleft: It was in January that she resigned.

Wh-Cleft (Pseudo-cleft)

Instead of it, you start with a what clause. This is called a wh-cleft or pseudo-cleft. It's especially useful when you want to emphasize what happened or what someone did.

What-cleft (forward)

What + [subject] + [verb] + be + [emphasized element]

  • What I need is a coffee. (Not tea, not water — a coffee.)
  • What happened was that the server crashed. (Explaining a problem.)
  • What she does best is handle difficult clients.

Reverse what-cleft

[Emphasized element] + be + what + [subject] + [verb]

  • A coffee is what I need.
  • The problem is what we need to address first.

Not-only-cleft (Inversion)

Less common but very effective for formal writing. When you start with Not only or Only after, you invert the subject and auxiliary.

Not only… but also

  • Normal: The system is reliable and it's fast.
  • Cleft: Not only is the system reliable, but it's also fast. (More emphatic.)

Only after / Only when

  • Normal: I understood the problem after reading the logs.
  • Cleft: Only after reading the logs did I understand the problem.
  • Only when you test with real users do you find the real issues.

When to Use (and When Not to)

Use cleft sentences when
  • You want to correct a misunderstanding: "It wasn't the budget — it was the timeline."
  • You're explaining a problem in detail: "What happened was…"
  • You want to sound more formal or persuasive in writing.
  • You're contrasting two possibilities.
Avoid cleft sentences when
  • The emphasis is already clear from context.
  • you're speaking casually — they can sound stiff.
  • overusing them — they're a spice, not the main dish.
  • in very short messages — "It was Tom who broke it" feels heavy for a Slack chat.
常见错误：很多学习者会造出 "It was the server that crashed yesterday it."It + be + 被强调部分 + that/who + 其余部分。没有第三个部分。
Who vs. That：强调人时，who 更正式、更自然；that 也可以。强调事物/时间/地点时，只能用 that（不能用 which）。例："It was the budget that caused the delay."
