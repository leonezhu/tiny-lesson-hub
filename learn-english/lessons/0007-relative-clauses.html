Relative Clauses — Which, That, Who, Whose

Lesson 7 · ~12 minutes · Grammar intermediate

By the end of this lesson, you'll know when to use who, which, that, and whose — and more importantly, when you can skip the relative word entirely. This is the grammar pattern that turns choppy, simple English into fluent, natural English.

What is a relative clause?

A relative clause is a mini-sentence embedded inside a bigger sentence that describes a noun. Think of it as a grammatical "glue" that lets you combine two short sentences into one smooth sentence. This is the single biggest difference between Chinese and English writing: Chinese strings clauses together by word order (意合), while English uses connector words like who, which, that, whose to mark the boundary (形合).

Two short sentences:
The woman is my manager. She lives next door.
↓ combine ↓
The woman who lives next door is my manager.

The four words you actually need

Who — for people

The person [who + verb] → "The person who called me is on her way."

  • The engineer who fixed the server works remotely.
  • Anyone who wants to join the project can sign up.
  • She's the colleague who always brings coffee.

Which — for things and ideas

The thing [which + verb] → "The system which we deployed last week is running."

  • The report which I sent you has three corrections.
  • We need a process which catches errors early.
  • The software which runs the website crashed at 3am.

Important: In American English, that is often preferred over which for restrictive clauses (see below). In British English, which is fine everywhere.

That — for people OR things (in restrictive clauses)

The person/thing [that + verb] → "The book that I read was great." / "The person that I met was helpful."

  • The best solution that we came up with was to delegate.
  • He's not the kind of person that gives up easily.
  • The email that came through this morning was confusing.

Whose — for possession (people, animals, things)

The person/thing [whose + noun] → "The company whose CEO resigned is being audited."

  • The developer whose code caused the bug was very apologetic.
  • That's the client whose account was hacked.
  • A building whose foundation was weak collapsed in the storm.
Hardest point for Chinese speakers: Chinese uses 的 for possession in relative clauses ("住隔壁的那个女人"), but English requires whose. Chinese has no word equivalent to "whose" — you just put 的 before the noun. So when you see possession + noun, think whose.

Restrictive vs. Non-restrictive (the comma rule)

This is the trickiest part, and here's the rule in plain English:

Restrictive (no commas): The clause defines which person/thing you mean. You cannot omit the relative word. Use that for things, who/that for people.

"The engineer who/that fixed the server is on leave." → There are multiple engineers; I'm specifying which one.

Non-restrictive (with commas): The clause just adds extra info. You must use which for things (not that), and you can omit the relative word if it's the object.

"The server, which was installed in 2019, crashed again." → There's only one server; the clause just adds a detail.

You CANNOT use "that" in a non-restrictive clause. "The server, that was installed..." is always wrong.
Commas are the marker: In Chinese, there are no commas to signal "extra info vs. essential info." This distinction doesn't exist in Chinese grammar. If you hear "the sentence has commas around the clause," think: non-restrictive → use which, not that.

When can you skip the relative word?

You can omit the relative word (who/which/that) only when it's the object of the relative clause — not the subject:

Subject position → CANNOT omit
The book [that/which I bought yesterday] is on my desk.
The book (that) I bought yesterday is on my desk. ← OK (object, can omit)
Subject position → CANNOT omit
The woman [who lives next door] is a doctor.
The woman (*who) lives next door is a doctor. ← WRONG to omit

Simple test: Is the relative word doing the action in the mini-sentence, or receiving the action?

  • The woman who lives next door → who does the living → subject → CANNOT omit
  • The book that I read → that is what I read → object → CAN omit
  • The report which we discussed → which is what we discussed → object → CAN omit

Relative word quick reference

When in doubt, use this decision tree:

Describing a person? → who (subject) / that (subject or object) / whom (formal object)
Describing a thing? → that (restrictive) / which (restrictive or non-restrictive)
Showing possession? → whose (person or thing)
Non-restrictive (has commas)? → which for things, who for people, NEVER that

Practice: Choose the Right Relative Word

Fill in each blank with the best relative word. There are 8 questions.

Q1
Describing a person (subject of the clause).
The engineer ________ designed the system left the company last month.
Q2
Describing a thing (object of the clause, can omit).
The report ________ I sent you has three errors.
Q3
Showing possession of a person.
That's the client ________ account was hacked.
Q4
Non-restrictive clause — extra info about a thing, with commas.
The server, ________ was installed in 2019, crashed again.
Q5
Describing a thing (restrictive clause, no commas).
The best solution ________ we came up with was to delegate.
Q6
Can you omit the relative word?
The woman ________ lives next door is a doctor. (Can "who" be omitted?)
Q7
Showing possession of a thing (company, building, etc.).
The company ________ CEO resigned is being acquired.
