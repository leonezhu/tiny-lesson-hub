Confusing Words: Affect, Effect, Effort, and More

Lesson 14 · ~10 minutes · Vocabulary precision

By the end of this lesson, you'll be able to confidently choose between the word pairs that trip up even B1 speakers: affect vs. effect, effect vs. affect, effort vs. affect, and several other high-frequency pairs.

Why this matters

Mandarin speakers regularly mix up affect/effect because both map loosely to 影响. But in English, one is a verb and the other is a noun — and using them wrong makes your writing look amateurish, even at B2 level. The same goes for effort/affort, principal/principle, and several other pairs that sound identical or similar but carry different grammatical roles.

关键原则： 英语里有一批"同音或近音词"，它们的意思不同、词性不同。中文翻译可能相同，但英语靠词性和固定搭配来区分。记住词性比记住中文翻译更有用。

The Top 6 Confusing Pairs

1. Affect (v.) vs. Effect (n.)
Affect — verb

To influence or change something.

  • The weather affected our travel plans. (影响 = 动词)
  • Stress can affect your health. (压力会影响健康)
  • How does this policy affect you? (这项政策如何影响你？)
Effect — noun

The result of a change.

  • The effect of the weather was huge. (影响 = 名词)
  • What effects did the policy have? (有什么影响？)
  • The medication had a side effect. (副作用)

Quick trick: Can you put "the" or "an" before it? If yes → effect (noun). If no → affect (verb).

⚠️ Rare exception: "Effect" can be a verb meaning "to bring about" (e.g., "to effect change"). "Affect" can be a noun in psychology (e.g., "flat affect"). These are advanced — ignore them for now.

2. Effect (as verb) vs. Affect (as verb)

Don't confuse these two very different verb patterns:

  • Affect = influence/change → "The data affected my decision." (影响)
  • Effect = cause to happen / implement → "The CEO effected major reforms." (实现/推行)

The phrase "effect change" is the most common — it means "bring change into reality." Not "influence change."

3. Effort (n.) vs. Affect (v.) — 发音相似

These two sound slightly similar (/ˈɛfərt/ vs. /əˈfɛkt/) and both relate to "influence," but:

  • Effort = noun: "It took a lot of effort." (努力 — 名词)
  • Affect = verb: "The data affected the outcome." (影响 — 动词)

Common mistake: "It takes a lot of affect" → WRONG. Should be "effort."

4. Principal vs. Principle

Sounds identical. Different meanings and word types.

  • Principal (adj. or n.) — first in rank; the head of a school.
  • The principal spoke at the assembly. (校长)
  • The principal reason was cost. (主要的)
  • Principle (n.) — a fundamental belief or rule.
  • She acted on principle. (出于原则)
  • It's one of my principles to be honest. (我的原则之一)

Quick trick: "Principal" = your pal (pal = friend). "Principle" ends in "-le" like "rule."

5. Lay vs. Lie

The most notorious confusion in English. The problem: "lay" can be the past tense of "lie" AND a separate verb entirely.

Lie (intransitive) — to recline, not putting something down
  • I lie down. (我去躺下 — 自己躺)
  • I lay down yesterday. (过去式: lie → lay)
  • I have lain here for hours. (过去分词: lie → lain)
Lay (transitive) — to put something down, requires an object
  • Please lay the book on the table. (把书放下 — 需要宾语)
  • She laid the keys down. (过去式: lay → laid)
  • The hen laid an egg. (母鸡下了一个蛋)

Quick trick: If there's something being put down (a book, keys, an egg) → lay/laid. If it's just "you" going to rest → lie/lay/lain.

6. Their / There / They're
  • Their = possessive → "That is their car." (他们的)
  • There = place → "Put it over there." (那里)
  • They're = "they are" → "They're coming later." (他们是)

Common email mistake: "Their going to the meeting" → should be "They're going."
