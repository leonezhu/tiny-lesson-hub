Idioms & Fixed Expressions (B2 Level)

Lesson 13 · ~10 minutes · Vocabulary — idiomatic English

By the end of this lesson, you'll be able to understand and use 10 common English idioms and fixed expressions at a B2 level — the kind that appear in meetings, emails, and everyday professional conversation. You'll also know when not to use them.

Why idioms matter at B2

At B1, you can communicate clearly with literal English. At B2, you start to sound natural — and natural English is full of idioms. Native speakers use them constantly, not to confuse you, but because they're efficient. Instead of saying "I understand completely," they say "I'm on the same page."

The challenge for Chinese speakers: many Chinese idioms (成语) don't translate literally into English. You can't just swap them one-for-one. You need to learn the English ones separately.

Category 1: Understanding & Agreement

1. Get the hang of it

"To get the handle of something"

To begin to understand or be able to do something after practice. Used when a skill feels difficult at first but becomes easier.

  • This software is complex, but you'll get the hang of it after a few sessions.
  • I couldn't drive on the motorway at first, but I've got the hang of it now.

💡 Common with "after a while," "after a bit," "once." Very friendly and encouraging tone.

2. See eye to eye

"To see with one eye aligned with another"

To agree completely with someone. Often used in the negative: "We don't see eye to eye."

  • We see eye to eye on most project decisions.
  • Unfortunately, the marketing and engineering teams don't always see eye to eye.

💡 More formal than "we agree." Common in professional contexts, especially when describing relationships.

3. On the same page

"All reading from the same book page"

To have the same understanding or information. Extremely common in business English.

  • Before we proceed, let's make sure we're all on the same page.
  • I sent the brief to everyone so the whole team is on the same page.

💡 This is the #1 idiom you'll hear in meetings. Safe to use in any professional context.

Category 2: Action & Progress

4. Bite off more than you can chew

"To take a bite of food larger than your mouth can handle"

To take on more work or responsibility than you can handle.

  • You've bitten off more than you can chew with three projects at once.
  • I think I bit off more than I could chew when I volunteered to lead the redesign.

💡 Usually used as advice or gentle criticism. Past tense: "bit off" (not "bite off").

5. Get the ball rolling

"To start something moving"

To start a process or activity.

  • Let's get the ball rolling with introductions.
  • I've drafted the proposal — now we just need to get the ball rolling.

💡 Very common meeting opener. Positive, energetic tone. Works in both spoken and written English.

6. Cut corners

"To shorten a path by cutting across"

To do something in the easiest, cheapest, or fastest way, often sacrificing quality.

  • If we cut corners on testing, bugs will slip through to production.
  • They cut corners on the build to meet the deadline, and now it's failing.

💡 Almost always negative. You warn against cutting corners or accuse others of doing so.

Category 3: Problems & Difficulties

7. Burn the midnight oil

"To burn oil lamps late into the night"

To work late into the night, especially to meet a deadline.

  • We've been burning the midnight oil all week to finish the presentation.
  • Don't burn the midnight oil tonight — you'll be exhausted tomorrow.

💡 Shows dedication but can also be a warning about overwork. Common in tech and creative industries.

8. Back to the drawing board

"Returning to where you planned something"

To start over because the previous attempt failed.

  • The client rejected the design. Back to the drawing board.
  • Our first prototype didn't work, so it's back to the drawing board for the engineering team.

💡 Can be said cheerfully (it's just a new chance) or frustratedly. Very common in creative and product work.

Category 4: Communication & Information

9. Play devil's advocate

"To argue against a position just to test it"

To argue the opposite side of an argument, not because you believe it, but to test the strength of the original position.

  • I actually agree with the plan, but let me play devil's advocate for a moment.
  • She always plays devil's advocate in meetings — it's annoying but makes our decisions stronger.

💡 A very sophisticated expression. Using it correctly signals strong English ability. Often used before presenting a counterpoint.

10. The bottom line

"The final line of a financial statement"

The most important factor; the final result. Originally from accounting (net profit = bottom line).

  • The bottom line is that we need to ship by Friday.
  • There were lots of details, but the bottom line is simple: the customer matters most.

💡 Used to summarize or cut through complexity. Very common in business and leadership communication.

关键区别：中文成语和英文习语不能直接翻译。"画蛇添足" 不等于 "paint the lily"（虽然意思接近）。每个语言有自己的固定表达，需要单独学习。不要试图直译中文成语到英语里。

When NOT to use idioms

Not every situation calls for an idiom. Here's when to be careful:

  • Formal reports and academic writing — avoid idioms entirely. Use literal language.
  • Legal documents — precision matters more than naturalness.
  • Writing to non-native speakers — idioms can confuse. Stick to clear, literal English.
  • Emails to senior stakeholders — depends on your relationship. When in doubt, skip the idiom.

Rule of thumb: If you wouldn't say it in a casual conversation with a colleague, don't write it in a formal document.

Practice: Choose the Right Idiom

Pick the idiom that fits the context. There are 8 questions.

Q1
Your team is struggling with a new tool. You want to encourage them.
It looks complicated, but you'll ________ after a few days.
Q2
A design was rejected. The team needs to start over.
The client didn't like the mockups. ________, I guess.
Q3
You want to summarize a long discussion quickly.
We've talked for an hour, but ________ is simple: we need more users.
Q4
Describing a disagreement between two departments.
Sales and Product don't always ________, but we manage.
Q5
Warning someone about taking on too much work.
Three projects plus a promotion campaign? You've ________.
Q6
Starting a meeting with introductions.
Alright everyone, let's ________ with quick round-table intros.
Q7
Offering to challenge a plan gently before implementing it.
I think the plan is solid, but let me ________ for a second.
Q8
Ensuring everyone has the same information before a decision.
Before we vote, let's make sure we're all ________,
