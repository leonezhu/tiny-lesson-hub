Conditionals: Real, Unreal & Mixed

Lesson 4 · ~12 minutes · Grammar intermediate

By the end of this lesson, you'll be able to use all three conditionals correctly in conversation and writing — and know when Chinese speakers tend to get them wrong.

Why this matters

Chinese doesn't have conditional verb forms. You express "if" with 如果 + statement, and the verb stays the same. In English, the verb changes depending on whether the situation is real, hypothetical, or impossible. This is one of the biggest gaps between B1 and B2.

The Three Conditionals

Zero Conditional — general truths & facts

If + present simple, present simple

  • If you heat water to 100°C, it boils.
  • If I skip sleep, I get headaches.
  • If the server goes down, the app crashes.

Use for things that are always true — scientific facts, habits, rules.

First Conditional — real future possibilities

If + present simple, will + base verb

  • If it rains tomorrow, I will bring an umbrella.
  • If you submit the report today, the client will review it.
  • She will be upset if you don't call her.

Use for specific future situations that could actually happen. Not "would" — use "will".

Second Conditional — unreal / unlikely present or future

If + past simple, would + base verb

  • If I had more time, I would learn guitar. (but I don't have time)
  • If she were the boss, she would change the policy. (she's not the boss)
  • If we lived closer, we would meet more often. (but we don't)

Use for imaginary or unlikely situations. Note: "If I were" (not "was") is preferred in formal English, though "was" is common in speech.

Common mistake for Chinese speakers: In Chinese, 如果下雨，我会带伞 uses the same verb form regardless of reality. In English: real possibility → "will", unreal/impossible → "would". Think: "will" = it might happen. "Would" = it's just a dream.

Mixed Conditionals (B2 bonus)

Sometimes the "if" clause refers to a past event and the result refers to now (or vice versa). Here's the most useful one:

Past cause → present result

If + past perfect, would have + past participle → result in present

If + past perfect, would + base verb

  • If I had studied engineering, I would be at Google now. (I didn't study engineering, so I'm not at Google now)
  • If she hadn't missed the flight, she would be here with us. (she missed it, she's not here)
Don't overthink mixed conditionals. The key question: "Is the 'if' part about the past or the present?" If past cause → present result: If I had done X (then), I would be Y (now). That's the one you'll use most.

Other things to know

"Unless" = "if ... not": "Unless you apologize, I won't forgive you" = "If you don't apologize..."
"Provided that" / "as long as" = "only if": "I'll lend you the car provided that you drive carefully."
Inversion (formal): "Were I you, I'd accept the offer." = "If I were you..."
No "if" needed in context: "I would help, but I'm busy." (implied: "if I weren't busy")

Practice: Choose the Right Conditional

There are 8 questions. Pick the answer that fits the context.

Q1
You're talking about a scientific fact.
If you mix red and blue, you ________ purple.
Q2
You're planning a meeting tomorrow. It might rain.
If it ________ tomorrow, we'll hold the meeting indoors.
Q3
You're daydreaming. You don't have a million dollars.
If I ________ a million dollars, I ________ around the world.
Q4
You regret not studying harder in university. You're thinking about your career now.
If I ________ harder at university, I ________ a better job now.
Q5
Giving advice informally.
If I ________ you, I'd accept the offer.
Q6
Talking about a rule at work.
If you ________ the wrong button, the system ________.
Q7
An unlikely future event — your company might go bankrupt.
If our company ________ bankrupt, we ________ looking for new jobs.
Q8
You're talking about something that didn't happen in the past, and its result in the past.
If she ________ the email, she ________ the deadline.
