Passive Voice: When & Why

Lesson 9 · ~10 minutes · Grammar foundation

By the end of this lesson, you'll know when to use passive voice in English, how to form it in any tense, and — most importantly — when to AVOID it so your writing sounds natural and professional.

What is Passive Voice?

In active voice, the subject does the action: "The manager approved the budget."
In passive voice, the subject receives the action: "The budget was approved by the manager."

The formula is always: be + past participle (V3). The "be" verb changes to match the tense.

Passive in Common Tenses

  • Present Simple: The report is written by the analyst. (not writes)
  • Past Simple: The decision was made last Friday.
  • Present Perfect: The contract has been signed.
  • Future (will): The results will be published next week.
  • Modal: The invoice should be paid within 30 days.
  • Present Continuous: The website is being redesigned.

When to Use Passive Voice

This is where most learners go wrong. They use passive all the time because in Chinese, the passive marker 被 is common — but in English, active voice is preferred 80-90% of the time. Use passive ONLY when it serves a purpose:

Use passive when...

  • You don't know who did it. "My laptop was stolen yesterday."
  • It doesn't matter who did it. "The new policy has been implemented across all offices."
  • You want to focus on the recipient, not the doer. "The CEO was interviewed by the BBC." (the CEO is the important person, not the BBC)
  • In scientific or formal writing, where objectivity matters. "The solution was heated to 80°C."
Chinese vs English: In Chinese, 被 is often used for negative events ("我被他骂了"). But in English, passive voice is NEUTRAL — it's not inherently negative. "The window was broken" doesn't mean anything bad happened; it's a factual statement. Don't over-avoid it. The key is: only use it when you have a reason.

Common Mistake: Active or Passive?

Here are two examples showing why using passive in the wrong situation makes your writing feel clunky and indirect:

Active (clear, direct, preferred)

  • "I completed the analysis and sent it to the client."
  • "The team discussed the issue in yesterday's meeting."
  • "We need to fix this bug before Friday."

Passive (awkward, indirect — unless you have a reason)

  • "The analysis was completed and was sent to the client." (who did it? Why not just say "I"?)
  • "The issue was discussed in yesterday's meeting." (okay — we don't care who discussed it)
  • "This bug needs to be fixed before Friday." (okay — focus is on the bug, not the fixer)
Watch out: In Chinese email writing, it's common to say "问题被解决了" (the problem was solved). In English business writing, just say "We've resolved the issue." Active voice sounds more confident and accountable.

By + Agent

When you DO use passive and need to mention who did it, use by — but only if it adds useful information:

  • "The meeting was rescheduled by the PM." (useful — tells you who decided)
  • "The report was written by a team of five analysts." (adds context)
  • "The email was sent." (no need to say "by me" — obviously)
