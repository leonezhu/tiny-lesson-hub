Email & Meeting English: Professional Tone

Lesson 15 · ~10 minutes · Professional communication

By the end of this lesson, you'll be able to write polite professional emails and participate in workplace meetings using the right register — moving from casual to appropriately formal without sounding robotic.

Why this matters

In Chinese workplaces, you can say "把这个给我看一下" to a colleague and it's perfectly normal. If you translate that directly to English, it sounds aggressive or rude. English has a built-in "politeness layer" — using modal verbs, indirect structures, and hedging words — that Chinese speakers often skip. This lesson covers the patterns you actually need at work.

Email Writing: Tone Spectrum

The biggest mistake non-native speakers make is writing emails that are either too blunt (like a command) or too stiff (like a legal contract). Here's the spectrum:

Good: Polite but natural

"Could you please take a look at the report when you get a chance?"

"I was wondering if we could schedule a quick call?"

Avoid: Too direct

"Send me the report."

"We need to have a call."

Key Email Patterns

1. Opening Lines

  • Formal: "I hope this email finds you well."
  • Semi-formal (most common): "Hope you're having a good week."
  • Casual (internal team): "Hi [name], quick question..."
  • Following up: "Just following up on my previous email about..."

2. Making Requests (The Politeness Ladder)

English requests have built-in gradations of politeness:

  • Direct: "Send me the file." (rude to anyone except close colleagues)
  • Softer: "Please send me the file." (acceptable in urgent situations)
  • Polite: "Could you send me the file?" (standard professional)
  • Very polite: "Would you mind sending me the file?" (when asking a favour)
  • Indirect: "I was wondering if you could possibly send me the file." (hedged, very safe)
Key insight: The word "could" is your best friend. "Could you..." works in 95% of work situations. It signals respect without sounding awkward.

3. Delivering Bad News or Pushback

Don't just say "no." Use the "softener + no + alternative" pattern:

  • "I understand the urgency, but we won't be able to meet that deadline. Could we aim for Friday instead?"
  • "That's an interesting idea. I'm not sure it's the right approach for this phase, though. What if we..."
  • "Unfortunately, we don't have the budget for that at the moment."

4. Email Sign-offs

  • Formal: "Best regards," "Kind regards,"
  • Semi-formal: "Best," "Thanks," "Many thanks,"
  • Casual: "Cheers," (UK/AU only, not US)
Common mistake: Avoid "Sincerely" unless it's a very formal cover letter. In day-to-day business, "Best regards" or just "Best" is the standard.

Meeting English

Meetings have their own vocabulary and patterns. Here are the most useful phrases for each phase:

Starting a Meeting / Taking the Floor

  • "Shall we get started?"
  • "Thanks everyone for joining. The purpose of today's meeting is..."
  • "I'd like to kick us off by going through..."
  • "Could I just jump in here?" (to interrupt politely)

Agreeing and Disagreeing

  • Agreeing: "I see your point." / "That makes sense." / "I'm with you on that."
  • Soft disagreement: "I see what you're saying, but..." / "I'm not sure I totally agree — have we considered...?" / "That's one way to look at it, but..."
  • Strong disagreement (careful): "I have to disagree with that." / "I'm afraid I can't support that approach."
The "I'm afraid" trick: Adding "I'm afraid" before a disagreement softens it naturally. "I'm afraid I disagree" sounds much softer than just "I disagree."

Clarifying and Checking Understanding

  • "Could you elaborate on that?"
  • "So, if I understand correctly, you're saying that..."
  • "Just to make sure I've got this right..."
  • "What do you mean by [term]?"

Wrapping Up

  • "So, the action items are..."
  • "I think we've covered everything. Shall we call it a day?"
  • "Let me summarise what we've decided."
  • "I'll follow up with an email recapping our decisions."

Common Mistakes from Chinese Speakers

1. No subject in requests: In Chinese you can say "能发一下文件吗？" (drop the subject). In English, "Can send the file?" is incomplete. Always include the subject: "Can you send the file?"

2. "Sorry" overuse: Chinese speakers often say "sorry" in contexts where English speakers wouldn't. In English, "sorry" implies fault. If you're just asking a question, say "Sorry to bother you" or better yet, "Quick question — can you help with...?"

3. Over-apologizing in emails: "I'm so sorry to disturb you again and again..." is too emotional for professional English. Use: "Following up on this" or "Just a quick reminder" instead.
